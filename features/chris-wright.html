Associate Professor Chris Wright is the Academic Director of Clinical Programs for Monash University's medical course. A/Prof Wright has a wealth of experience as an intensive care physician, programmer, and educator. We were very fortunate to have the chance to ask A/Prof Wright about his experiences in both medicine and programming.

How did you first begin programming?

The first time I learnt to code was at school in 1971. There was a language called MINITRAN which was a subset of Fortran. You'd code it with punch cards which had little squares that you could push out with a paperclip. You first had to punch out all the commands and numbers, then you submitted it, and two weeks later you'd get a printout which either had one line that said e.g. "Syntax error at line 8", or it ran your code. That was my first exposure to coding.

Tell us about your experiences with programming.

In University

When I was in university, I was somehow able to get an account on the computer - so in 1975, I had an account at Melbourne University on the RSTS/E runtime system. The internet was basically a cable that came through the wall back then - it was before the web, but we did have email. I used that computer to program things like Conway's Game of Life in RSTS Basic. At first we just had printouts, so you'd be coding at a Teletype - a typewriter - and when you ran your code, it would print out one line and your pattern would come out, then it would think, and the next pattern would come out.

Then we got terminals, and that was a big deal. And the editor I used there was ed, which was pre-vi, and you got quite good at remembering how many lines you had to jump down and delete.

With that, I coded a subset of Guyton's circulatory physiology model, and that's where I started to get really interested in what mathematical modelling might have to do with medicine. Guyton's model allows you to make simple changes to the circulation, yet have quite complex insights. It's a good example of a spherical cow, where you're ignoring all the ephemera and just concentrating on the core issues.

But if I put this model in front of a group of final-year medical students or even intensive care registrars, I can ask questions of this model which they can't answer. There's no pulsatility in the heart in the model and it's got no lungs - and you might think "How is that relevant? How can that possibly be helpful?" It turns out, if you model the right elements of a system, you can gain deep insights into the system. It showed me that a complicated model isn't always the best model. So that was very interesting, and I just solved it with Eulerian integration.

Career

Then I finished medicine after a couple of years, and thought about going into computer science for good because there was a bit of a push for it in Melbourne at the time. But I decided to keep going. I bought an Osborne 1 - that was my first computer - and programmed mostly in assembly language and Forth. And Forth is a beautiful language, and should have been around longer. Around that time, there was also a book called Godel, Escher, Bach by Douglas Hofstadter, which used Lisp as its language in parts, and that got me interested in Lisps.

I went through a succession of computers, including Osbornes and Apples and Kaypros. Then in 1984 I had a 110-volt Mac - one of the first in the country - which you'd program with Apple Pascal.

Soon after, I finished my nephrology training. I did some study at RMIT in artificial intelligence, then went to intensive care medicine. And in my intensive care work, I worked for 25 years with an intensive care specialist named Geoff Parkin.

Geoff is one of the most innovative and brilliant physiologists I've ever met, and he and I spent 25 years doing a whole lot of computer work in intensive care. Much of this work was based around open-loop and closed-loop control of the circulatory and respiratory systems - taking information out of the monitors, processing it, then providing therapy directly with or without a human involved. We also did a fair amount of simulation work. Windows wasn't my favourite world, but Windows had COM objects back then, and the COM objects provided quite good interfaces to serial devices and analog devices so you didn't have to do the lower-level driver writing.

I was also involved in the Health Informatics Society of Australia. In fact, last year the 5 or 6 of us who were around at the start were made life members. That was nice.

What are your thoughts and experiences on how programming applies to medicine?

Here's an example - there was a program called MYCIN which came out of Stanford. It was a rules-based expert system for infectious diseases. And in the 80's, they published a paper showing that its advice was about as good as ID consultants.

So the question we have to ask is why aren't we all using it? At least 30 years ago we knew how well computers could do this, so what happened? We're starting to use computer systems to help us now and antimicrobial stewardship is a big deal, but we've known we could use computers to help for about 30 years. I think this really highlights that the cultural context in which we put technology is important, and you can't win with good technology alone. You've got to take people with you.

People went off AI back then - it was the "AI winter", AI had overpromised and underdelivered - but as soon as the commercial imperative became obvious, AI got a role again. And people started getting interested in how we might use knowledge to improve care or at least save money. Since then there's been a real growth in statistical AI and machine learning. And we're starting to see AI having a real effect on healthcare now. It's starting to become an okay thing to talk about.

But I think one of the risks is that when people say AI in medicine, they're talking about neural networks and machine learning - but there's a whole world of symbolic reasoning. Its got its limitations, but so has machine learning. I think some hybridisation of both these models will turn out to be good.

And there's also all these other areas - like HL-7 - which are less "sexy", but a requirement for good healthcare systems. The idea behind systems like HL-7 is so "Device A" can talk to "Device B" through a shared taxonomy. It's a very important idea - people need to work on it - but most people get hung up on things like AlphaGo.

What interests you about programming?

I think it started with Guyton's model. I've always liked maths - and here was a real chance to do experimental mathematics. Writing some maths and seeing it run was very appealing and quite engaging; you could increase the systemic vascular resistance and watch the blood pressure go up! I thought that if I can make a computer model of a problem run, then to some extent I understand the problem. That was the key for me.

I also like solving puzzles, which often involve finding how to represent things parsimoniously. And there's the joy of representing something about the world in a format which is executable. What you've done is made something explicit so other people can share your mindset.

And it turns out I like programming languages. I was involved in Python back in the early days and went to an early Python conference in San Diego. Python struck me as a very interesting language early on, and we wrote a lot of the ICU code in Python as well. Mark Hammond wrote a fantastic interface to the COM world so you could use Python to call COM objects back in the day.

I think I also enjoy the intellectual insights that very clever people have had, and wonder why we just sort of hack away at JavaScript. For example, Bill Atkinson wrote HyperCard - which I think is the greatest computer system ever written. All those lessons have been lost, and that makes me a bit sad.

What is your advice for students interested in programming and medicine?

My advice for this group of people - and they're very important people because they'll make a big contribution - is if they're going to do computer science, they shouldn't do information procesing. They need to not come out at as JavaScript or Java hackers. They need to do either electrical engineering - in which case they'll learn Matlab, assembly, chips, and will make a huge contribution to learning to make better and new devices - or they need to do computer science in the SICP sense - not in a business information sense. So they shouldn't be taught how to use a language by a course where you'll come out knowing how to program in Java. That's not what's important for innovation.

I think there's great merit to learning the stuff that was known in the "old days". I'm a big fan of Alan Kay - Alan Kay worked at Xerox PARC, where they invented object-oriented programming, laser printers and the ethernet. Smalltalk came out of PARC.

Alan has a set of fantastic YouTube videos where he talks about software engineering not being engineering yet. He says software engineering is a bit like engineering before we discovered the arch. What we did with the pyramids was pile bricks on top of one another - but once we discovered the arch, it became a principle that allowed us to build efficiently and reliably.

He's also highly critical of what he calls "pop culture computer science" - of which there could not possibly be a better example than programming for the web in JavaScript. Fortunately, people are starting to get on top of that and languages like Elm are starting to have an effect. But it is amazing what we've forgotten, and if you get a Smalltalk system out from the 90s and have a look at what you could do, it just makes you weep when you compare it to trying to work out how to center a <div>.

So students need to have some historical perspectve too. I thnk one of the issues is that we're purely future-looking - back in the day, we already knew how to do many things. Elm's a good example - Elm has reinvented Haskell for the web. And JavaScript was an attempt to get Scheme on the web. So I would encourage these people to look at the old stuff, not just the new stuff.

What programming languages have you used and what is your favourite?

I always have trouble with this; the language I can get something done best with is Python, but the language I enjoy programming the most with is Scheme.

Since my interest has always been a bit more in the symbolic reasoning area, the languages I've used have always been languages like Lisp, Scheme, Forth and Smalltalk rather than the compiled C world. In particular, there are two great books - Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs (SICP), and Paradigms of AI Programming (PAIP) - and they're the two best programming books I've ever read. I really enjoyed understanding their ideas, and they were both written using Lisps - SICP in Scheme and PAIP in Common Lisp.

There's another language I've used and liked called Tcl, though it's dying. The reason it's an amazing language is that Tk is a graphical toolkit and Tcl is a scripting language. And it was originally written to be embedded in a C program - so you had your compiled C program running and you embedded the Tcl/Tk interpreters and libraries in there, and you could call a GUI and script your program in Tcl/Tk. It's available on all sorts of platforms, and still maintained and still powerful. I don't use it much now, but I have used it in the past.

What's your favourite Lisp dialect?

Racket. I think Racket is a wonderful language, and its libraries are very "batteries-included" and complete. There's a good article that I think is a bit amusing - Racket is an acceptable Python. Racket's macro system is amazing. Common Lisp macros are straightforward and powerful but they're "non-hygienic" - you can get into trouble with bindings being captured and scope problems can arise. Racket's macros, done well, guarantee a whole lot properties like composability and hygiene.

What would you recommend for medical students beginning to learn programming?

I would be put between Python and Scheme. I would want the syntax to get out of the way as soon as possible and for there to be minimal surprises. Scheme has no syntax almost, but the brackets put people off a bit. Python has a few surprises with scope and default parameters.

I'd give people Visual Studio Code - much as I hate to say it - and install Python, and Racket (with the DrRacket UI). I'd get them to produce a function like an adder. So you're passing around a function.

For example, in Racket, you can do it like so: 

#lang racket

(define (adder n)
  (lambda (m) (+ n m))
)

So if I say: 

(define plus-two (adder 2))

And then say: 

(plus-two 3)

I get 5. So plus-two has returned a function which is closed over n. And if I can get medical students to understand that piece of code, I think we're getting somewhere.

So that's what I think we ought to teach - that's the type of computer science I think medical students should learn. And if you learn that stuff, you can pick up languages, that's not a problem. And I think the particulars like how your IDE works are just ephemera.

What's your favourite editor? Favourite OS?

My favourite editor is Emacs - except if I'm editing text. I'm a great believer of "Emacs is a great operating system, lacking only a decent text editor". I use Emacs plus evil - so I get Vim for my text editing and Emacs for its customizability and all its modes. And if you're going to program in Lisps, Emacs just works better. Org mode is also powerful in the Emacs world - I'm a believer in reproducible research, and I think that's one of the key issues in medicine, so I try to write any analysis code in an org mode document with org-babel.

Evan Misschula has a wonderful video on YouTube where he discussed the crisis in research that is pushing the reproducible research project. Everyone involved in research and the use and development of tools needs to watch this.

In terms of OS - MacOS. It gives me solid apps that I have to use in my work, and they just work - Word works, Excel work, Powerpoint works. Some people don't like these programs for various reasons, but I think Excel, for example, is a very good spreadsheet. And LibreOffice just doesn't cut it for me. I've had Linux boxes in the past for example, using Slackware - but Mac just gets the right balance for me between a Unix environment and a productive office environment.
